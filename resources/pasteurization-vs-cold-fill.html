Pasteurization vs. Cold-Fill — Pick One

A practical decision tree based on pH, sugar content, and distribution channel. We make the call on every formulation we run — here's how we think about it.

📖 5 min read · Good Liquid Bev Co

Every beverage we co-pack lives or dies on its processing decision. Pasteurize it and you get shelf stability, extended distribution reach, and room-temperature retail placement. Cold-fill it and you preserve delicate flavors, live cultures, and a "clean" ingredient story — but you are now chained to refrigerated logistics for the product's entire life. This is not a stylistic choice. It is a fundamental business decision, and the wrong one will cost you.

The Basics: What Each Method Does

Tunnel pasteurization

After the product is canned or bottled, the sealed container passes through a warm water tunnel. The standard target is 140–149°F for a specified time — measured in Pasteurization Units (PUs). The formula is:

PU = time (minutes) × 1.393(T − 60)

Where T is temperature in degrees Celsius. A typical commercial target is 10–25 PUs for beer; high-acid products like kombucha often target 5–15 PUs.

Tunnel pasteurization kills or inactivates pathogenic and spoilage organisms, yielding a product that is shelf-stable at ambient temperature for 12–18 months when properly sealed.

Cold-fill / cold packaging

The product is kept cold throughout — typically below 39°F — from the end of production through the entire distribution chain. No heat is applied. Microbiological stability depends on a combination of pH, sugar content (water activity), dissolved CO₂, and alcohol content rather than thermal kill.

Product Type Mapping

✓ Typically Pasteurized
  • Hard seltzer (ABV 4–8%)
  • Craft beer (canned)
  • Fruit juice blends
  • RTD cocktails (low ABV)
  • Sparkling water with juice
  • Non-alcoholic beer
✓ Typically Cold-Fill
  • Live-culture kombucha
  • Probiotic beverages
  • Cold brew coffee
  • Unpasteurized juice (HPP or cold-fill)
  • Kefir-based drinks
  • High-ABV products (14%+)
Edge case — hard kombucha: Hard kombucha is a hybrid. The alcohol (0.5–8% ABV) combined with pH typically in the 3.0–3.5 range provides strong microbial hurdles. Some hard kombucha brands do pasteurize to prevent over-fermentation in the package. If you keep live cultures in your hard kombucha, you must cold-fill and manage ABV creep risk. This is not a both-and situation.

The Cold Chain Cost Reality

Cold-fill sounds simple until you price out what a full cold chain actually costs. Every node in your distribution network must be refrigerated:

  • Co-packer cold storage: Many facilities charge $0.25–$0.75 per case per month for refrigerated warehouse space.
  • Refrigerated freight: Reefer truck rates typically run 25–40% higher than dry van for equivalent mileage.
  • Distributor cold storage: Not all distributors have adequate refrigerated capacity. This limits your distributor options to those who specialize in refrigerated beverages.
  • Retail cooler placement: You are competing for limited cooler-door slots. Ambient shelf brands face no such constraint.
Margin impact: A brand moving to national distribution via cold chain often finds that refrigerated logistics adds $1.50–$3.00 per case in freight cost versus ambient. At 200 cases per month, that is $300–$600 of recurring margin erosion before you've accounted for higher storage costs.

High-Pressure Processing: A Third Path

High-Pressure Processing (HPP) is worth mentioning as an alternative for products where both pasteurization and cold-fill create problems. HPP uses cold water pressure (87,000 psi) to inactivate pathogens without heat, extending refrigerated shelf life to 60–120 days while preserving flavor and live cultures. The tradeoffs:

  • HPP is a toll processing service — you ship your filled, sealed product to an HPP facility, which adds logistics complexity and cost ($0.08–$0.15 per unit)
  • HPP still requires cold chain — it extends refrigerated shelf life, but the product is not shelf-stable at ambient temperature
  • Not all packaging is HPP-compatible — soft-sided pouches and some cap types cannot withstand the pressure cycle

HPP makes the most sense for juice blends, probiotic beverages, and ready-to-drink products where clean label, live cultures, and extended cold shelf life are all required simultaneously.

How We Make the Call at Good Liquid

When a new brand comes to us with a formulation, we walk through four questions in order:

  1. What is the product's pH? — We test every batch. Anything above 4.6 requires pasteurization; below 4.6 gives us options.
  2. What does the label claim require? — "Contains live cultures" or "probiotic" means cold-fill. No exceptions.
  3. Where does this product sell? — If the brand's target is Whole Foods cooler doors, cold-fill works. If they're going into vending, food service, or club stores, we need shelf stability.
  4. Can the founder support a cold chain at their volume? — A 200-case/month brand shipping refrigerated product nationally is fighting a logistics battle with one hand tied behind their back. We'll have that conversation honestly before they commit to cold-fill.
