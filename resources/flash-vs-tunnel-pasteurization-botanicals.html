Flash Pasteurization vs. Tunnel Pasteurization — Why It Matters for Botanical Beverages

Both methods achieve the same microbial kill. But one does it in 15 seconds with precise temperature control. The other exposes your lavender, hibiscus, and terpenes to 25 minutes of uncontrolled heat — and the science shows exactly what that costs you.

📖 7 min read · Good Liquid Bev Co

When a brand comes to us with a botanical RTD — hibiscus seltzer, lavender lemonade, chamomile sparkling tea, a hopped hard kombucha — the first processing conversation we have is about pasteurization method. Not because one method is right and the other is wrong for every product, but because the choice matters enormously when your selling proposition is the integrity of a delicate plant ingredient. Tunnel pasteurization and flash pasteurization both achieve food safety. What they do to your botanicals in the process is a completely different story.

The Key Scientific Concept: Thermolability

The word you need to know is thermolabile. A thermolabile compound is any chemical compound that loses its biological activity, flavor properties, color, or molecular structure when exposed to heat. The counterpart — compounds that are stable under heat — are called thermostable.

The vast majority of the compounds that make botanical beverages worth drinking are thermolabile:

Hibiscus / Elderberry
Anthocyanins
Pigment molecules. Color and antioxidant activity degrade under sustained heat. Losses accelerate above 167°F.
Lavender / Chamomile
Linalool, α-Bisabolol
Key aromatic terpenes. Linalool dropped from 93% to 27% of its original concentration after sustained heat exposure in a controlled lab study.
Citrus Botanicals
Limonene, Myrcene
Volatile terpenes begin evaporating well below boiling point. Oxidize into off-flavor compounds under prolonged heat.
Ginger / Turmeric
Gingerols, Curcuminoids
Bioactive compounds. Curcumin degrades under sustained alkaline heat; gingerols convert to less potent shogaols under prolonged heating.
Green / White Tea
Catechins, EGCG
Polyphenolic flavonoids. Begin losing activity at the lower end of tunnel pasteurization temperature ranges (140°F).
Any Citrus / Berry
Ascorbic Acid (Vit. C)
Food scientists describe this as "the most thermolabile compound" in pasteurized juice products. Measurable losses begin at pasteurization temperatures.

None of these compounds are destroyed instantaneously. Their degradation is a function of two variables: temperature and time. The longer a thermolabile compound is held at elevated temperature, the more of it you lose — and the loss accelerates exponentially as temperature rises. This is the core tension in botanical beverage pasteurization: achieving microbial safety (which requires heat) without destroying the plant compounds that make the product worth buying.

The Outside-In Problem: Why Tunnel Temperature Is "All Over the Place"

Tunnel pasteurization heats a sealed container, not a liquid stream. Hot water is sprayed on the exterior of the can or bottle; heat then conducts inward through the aluminum wall (which is thin and conducts quickly) and then through the liquid (which is far slower). The liquid is not stirred during this process.

The result is a significant and unavoidable temperature gradient inside every container:

To guarantee the cold spot reaches 140°F, the rest of the liquid must already be hotter. The liquid near the top of the can — which heats first from the sprayed water above — can reach 167–185°F or higher during tunnel pasteurization. These are temperatures that fall well into the accelerated degradation range for anthocyanins, terpenes, and polyphenols. And unlike flash pasteurization's 15-second exposure, this temperature is maintained for 20–30 minutes.

Tunnel pasteurization is designed around the cold spot — the slowest-to-heat point in the container. By definition, every other point in the liquid runs hotter than the target, for the full dwell time. There is no way to prevent this with a tunnel system. It is a physical consequence of heating a sealed container from the outside.

The Pasteurization Unit Math — Why Flash Achieves Equivalent Safety in 15 Seconds

The standard measure of pasteurization effectiveness in the beverage industry is the Pasteurization Unit (PU). One PU equals one minute of heating at 140°F (the industry reference temperature). The formula, derived from the Z-value of brewing pathogens, is:

PU = t × 1.393 ((T − 140) / 1.8)
where t = time in minutes · T = temperature in °F · Z-value = 12.5°F · 1.393 = lethality rate constant
Tunnel @ 140°F for 20 minutes20 × 1.393⁰ = 20 PU
Flash @ 162°F for 15 seconds (0.25 min)0.25 × 1.393¹² ≈ ~17 PU
Flash @ 165°F for 15 seconds (0.25 min)0.25 × 1.393¹⁴ ≈ ~32 PU
Industry standard target (most beverages)15–25 PU

The math confirms what the physics already tells us: 15 seconds at 162–165°F delivers equivalent or greater microbial kill as 20+ minutes at 140°F. This is not magic — it is the exponential relationship between temperature and pathogen death rate. Harmful organisms die far more rapidly at higher temperatures; the Z-value of 12.5°F means that every 12.5°F rise in temperature produces a 10-fold increase in lethality.

Here is the critical asymmetry: pathogens obey this exponential relationship far more steeply than most thermolabile botanical compounds do. Microorganisms die very rapidly at 162°F; terpene evaporation and anthocyanin degradation, while temperature-dependent, do not accelerate at the same rate. This asymmetry is the entire scientific basis for flash pasteurization's preservation advantage. You are exploiting the fact that microbes are more sensitive to high temperature than your botanicals are — and you use that window to kill the microbes before significant botanical damage accumulates.

The core insight: Both methods achieve 15–25 PU. Flash pasteurization gets there in 15 seconds at a precisely controlled temperature. Tunnel pasteurization gets there in 20–30 minutes at a variable, non-uniform temperature — and your thermolabile botanical compounds are exposed to every second of it.

Dialing In Temperature: The Flash Pasteurizer Advantage

Modern flash (HTST) pasteurizers maintain temperature tolerance of ±0.5–0.9°F through closed-loop control systems. A flow diversion valve (FDV) continuously monitors the temperature at the end of the holding tube. If the temperature drops even fractionally below setpoint, the FDV diverts the product stream back to the balance tank before any under-pasteurized liquid can reach the filler. Every unit of product that passes to filling has been confirmed at target temperature for the full required hold time.

This level of control means a botanical beverage formulator can do something tunnel pasteurization simply cannot offer: recipe-specific temperature dialing.

  • A linalool-forward lavender seltzer might be run at 162°F for 15 seconds — the minimum required for the target PU — to minimize terpene evaporation.
  • A high-acid hibiscus kombucha with known pH 3.2 and 5% ABV might be run at 161°F (the FDA minimum), leveraging its combined hurdles (pH + alcohol + carbonation) to achieve safety at the lowest possible thermal load.
  • A lower-acid turmeric ginger RTD with less inherent microbial protection might be run at 165–167°F for a fuller PU margin — but still for only 15–30 seconds, not 25 minutes.

In a tunnel pasteurizer, the temperature is set for the product on the belt. The belt speed and zone temperatures are the levers. There is no real-time liquid temperature measurement. There is no FDV to catch off-spec product. And there is no capacity to dial in a botanical-specific thermal profile — every product running through the tunnel that day gets the same heat treatment, applied from the outside, for the same dwell time.

Precision matters: For a botanical formulation where the difference between 162°F and 163°F can meaningfully affect terpene retention, a ±0.5°F flash system gives you real control. The tunnel gives you hot water and a timer.

Botanical Beverages That Are Most at Risk in a Tunnel

If your product has any of the following, tunnel pasteurization deserves extra scrutiny:

  • Color from anthocyanin-rich botanicals (hibiscus, elderberry, butterfly pea flower, purple carrot) — visual color shift from heat degradation is measurable and customer-facing
  • Volatile aromatics as the primary flavor driver (lavender, rose, yuzu, bergamot, any citrus forward blend) — the aroma compounds that make these botanicals distinctive are exactly the ones that evaporate under prolonged heat
  • Live cultures — tunnel pasteurization eliminates them entirely; if probiotic count is a label claim, tunnel is categorically incompatible
  • Vitamin C as a label claim — "excellent source of Vitamin C" on a tunnel-pasteurized product is a regulatory and quality risk; post-process Vitamin C degradation during the 20–30 minute heat exposure is well-documented
  • Tea-forward formulations — catechin degradation begins at 140°F; EGCG is among the most thermolabile polyphenols
  • Low sugar / low acid products — these have fewer natural hurdles, so they require higher PU targets; achieving higher PU in a tunnel means even longer exposure or higher temperature, amplifying compound loss

When Tunnel Pasteurization Is the Right Call

To be clear: tunnel pasteurization is not wrong for every product. It has real advantages in certain contexts:

  • High-gravity, thermostable products — a standard malt-forward beer with no botanical adjuncts, no color-sensitive pigments, and no live culture claims is a reasonable tunnel candidate. Hop iso-alpha acids are more thermostable than most botanical terpenes.
  • Existing packaging infrastructure — if a co-packer's entire line is built around tunnel pasteurization and you need to move quickly, the tunnel may be the practical choice for a first run while you evaluate next steps.
  • Post-fill contamination risk — because tunnel pasteurization treats the sealed container, it provides a final kill step that also addresses any potential post-fill contamination of the package exterior. HTST-then-fill relies entirely on the integrity of the aseptic or clean-fill environment.

But for any beverage where the botanical compounds are the product — where the lavender has to taste like lavender, where the hibiscus has to be visually red, where the green tea has to carry its catechin content to the shelf — flash pasteurization's combination of precise temperature control, short hold time, and recipe-specific dialing is the scientifically correct choice.

The bottom line: Tunnel pasteurization doesn't know what's inside your can. It heats the outside and hopes the inside reaches the target. Flash pasteurization treats your liquid directly, at a temperature you set, for a time you control, and confirms every drop before it goes into the package. For thermolabile botanical formulations, that difference is the difference between a product that tastes like the plant it's named after and one that doesn't.

How We Handle Botanical Formulations at Good Liquid

We use batch flash pasteurization for every botanical beverage we produce. Before a new formulation runs, we evaluate four factors together:

  1. pH of the finished beverage — lower pH provides natural microbial protection and allows us to set the minimum required PU target rather than a conservative overcorrection
  2. Alcohol content (if applicable) — ABV contributes to the combined hurdle effect; a 5% ABV hard kombucha with pH 3.2 requires far fewer PUs than a 0.5% ABV non-alcoholic chamomile tea
  3. Thermolability profile of the botanical blend — which compounds are we trying to preserve? Anthocyanins? Volatile terpenes? Ascorbic acid? Each has a different sensitivity window that informs our temperature setpoint.
  4. Target shelf life and distribution channel — required PU target is a function of how long the product needs to be stable and at what temperature it will be stored

The goal is always the same: the minimum thermal load required to achieve safe, shelf-stable product — no more, no less. For a hibiscus-lavender seltzer with pH 3.0 and 5.5% ABV, that might be 161°F for 15 seconds. For a low-acid chamomile RTD with no alcohol, it might be 165°F for 20 seconds. We set the temperature for each formulation, not for "whatever's running today."
