How to Launch a Hard Kombucha Brand

From recipe to retail in 5 milestones. What to expect on cost, timeline, and the regulatory paperwork no one warns you about.

📖 6 min read · Good Liquid Bev Co

Hard kombucha sits at an interesting crossroads: it is simultaneously a craft fermented beverage and a regulated alcohol product. That dual identity means founders have to satisfy both the health-food buyer at Whole Foods and the federal Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB). Most first-timers underestimate the second part. This guide walks you through the five milestones that take a kombucha brand from concept to store shelf, with real numbers at every step.

The short version: Budget 12–18 months and $75,000–$150,000 to get your first pallet to retail. If those numbers feel big, co-packing (what we do) is how you compress both.
01

Nail the formula and get it approved

Hard kombucha must reach at least 0.5% ABV to be regulated as alcohol. Most brands target 3.5%–7% ABV so they sit in the beer/cider aisle rather than requiring a liquor license at retail. Your first formulation milestone is threefold:

  • Recipe development — base tea, SCOBY culture, sugar source, flavor additions, and secondary fermentation control. Consistency across batches is harder than it sounds; professional lab analysis on every pilot batch is non-negotiable.
  • TTB formula approval — if your product contains any fruit juice, natural flavors, or adjuncts (herbs, honey, etc.), you must submit a formula to TTB's Formulation Section and receive approval before you may sell commercially. This takes 30–90 days on a standard submission; budget extra time.
  • COLA (Certificate of Label Approval) — once your formula is approved, every label variant must be submitted and approved before the product ships across state lines. Plan for 45–75 days per submission.
Watch out: ABV creep is the #1 compliance risk in hard kombucha. Live cultures continue fermentation after packaging. Your co-packer must validate that your product does not continue fermenting significantly in the package — or you could end up shipping a product that tests over your stated ABV at retail.
02

Obtain your federal Brewer's Notice (or use a licensed co-packer)

Any entity that produces malt beverages or fermented beverages with more than 0.5% ABV must hold a federal Brewer's Notice from the TTB. This is a free permit but requires:

  • Completion of TTB Form 5130.10 (Brewer's Notice)
  • Background check on all principals with 10%+ ownership
  • Bond requirement for expected excise tax liability
  • State brewery license in your state of production (separate from federal; timeline and cost vary widely)

If you co-pack with a licensed facility like Good Liquid, production happens under our Brewer's Notice, not yours. You still need TTB approval for your formula and label, but you skip the brewery licensing process entirely — often saving 4–6 months of lead time.

03

Packaging: cans, carriers, and minimums

12-ounce slim cans are the category standard for hard kombucha. You will need to decide between:

  • Brite cans + printed shrink sleeves — lower MOQ (you can start around 150–200 cases), slower line speed, slightly less premium shelf look
  • End-printed (CDL) cans — requires 3,000–5,000 case minimum orders from the can supplier; lower per-unit cost at scale; cleanest retail presentation

PakTech 4-pack and 6-pack carriers are the de facto multipack format in the category. They run roughly $0.17–$0.22 per carrier and are available in 30+ colors to match your brand palette. See our separate guide on PakTech and custom lid colors for cost breakdowns.

04

Distribution: three-tier system 101

In the United States, alcohol follows the three-tier system: producer → distributor → retailer. As a new brand you almost certainly cannot sell directly to retailers in most states. You need a licensed distributor in each state where you sell.

Early-stage strategy most brands use:

  • Self-distribution — allowed in some states for small producers; keeps more margin but requires a vehicle, warehouse, and your own sales effort
  • Regional craft distributor — the right fit for most emerging brands in year one; look for a distributor that specializes in craft or better-for-you beverages, not one that primarily handles macro beer
  • Statewide expansion — sign distribution agreements in 2–4 states in year one, not 20; spread too thin and you can't support your distributor with sales reps and programming
Margin math: Producer → distributor markup is typically 25–30%. Distributor → retailer is another 30–35%. If your retail price is $14.99 for a 4-pack, you may receive $7.50–$9.00 from your distributor. Build your cost structure around that number, not the retail price.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a separate FDA registration?

Because hard kombucha is an alcoholic beverage (>0.5% ABV), it is regulated by TTB, not the FDA. However, if your product is below 0.5% ABV (non-alcoholic kombucha), FDA rules apply and you will need a food facility registration. This distinction matters enormously for labeling requirements — Supplement Facts panel vs. TTB Alcohol Facts statement.

Can I produce hard kombucha at home to test before scaling?

Federal law prohibits home production of alcoholic beverages for commercial purposes. You can develop and refine your recipe at home for personal consumption (as the law allows), but you cannot sell, distribute, or even give away home-produced hard kombucha. For commercial recipe development, work with a licensed pilot brewery or co-packer.

How do I protect my formula?

Beverage formulas are generally not patentable, but they can be protected as trade secrets. Have any co-manufacturer sign a non-disclosure agreement before sharing your formula. Co-packers like Good Liquid routinely sign NDAs and work with dozens of brands — protecting your formula is standard practice, not a favor.

What certifications do hard kombucha brands usually pursue?

Common certifications sought by brands in this space include USDA Organic (for organic ingredients), Non-GMO Project Verified, and Gluten-Free (important: most kombuchas start with a gluten-free base, but cross-contact must be validated). Each certification has its own audit and annual fee structure. Discuss with your co-packer which certifications their facility already holds, as this can dramatically reduce your certification cost.
